Best Places To View Wildlife in Washington State

Lime Kiln Point State Park


1567 West Side Road
Friday Harbor, WA 98250
(360) 902-8844
www.parks.wa.gov

Lime Kiln Point State Park, also known as “Whale Watch Park” for its abundance of cetaceans, is world renowned as one of the best places to watch whales and other wildlife. The park spans 36 acres of diverse terrain, with forested highlands, rocky shoreline, and expansive beaches. The park also has an iconic lighthouse constructed in 1919 that still helps ships navigate through the Haro Strait. In addition to seeing orca and minke whales, visitors can also catch a glimpse of seals and sea lions, porpoises, bald eagles and other sea birds, and otters.

Mount Saint Helens Wildlife Area


Cougar, WA 98616
(360) 902-2515
www.wdfw.wa.gov

Thirty-five years ago, Mount Saint Helens was the fifth-tallest peak in Washington State, but a catastrophic eruption forever changed its landscape and its inhabitants. The Washington State Department of Game estimates that almost 7,000 large animals died in the eruption and ensuing lahars. More than three decades later, the nutrient-rich volcanic soil has borne lush plant life and seen a return of many native animal species. The Mount Saint Helens Wildlife Area, which includes 3,940 acres, provides opportunities to view ungulates, including deer and elk; cougars; coyotes; amphibians, including Pacific tree frogs and western toads; reptiles, including northern alligator lizards and northwestern garter snakes; waterfowl; birds of prey; and songbirds.

Deception Pass State Park


41229 State Route 20
Oak Harbor, WA 3767
(360) 675-2417
www.parks.wa.gov

Crowned by the expansive Deception Pass Bridge, Deception Pass State Park encompasses more than 4,100 acres and includes 77,000 feet of saltwater shoreline. Deception Pass Bridge, which actually includes two bridges that span Deception Pass to the south and Canoe Pass to the north, connects Whidbey Island to the Fidalgo Islands. The bridge, which was completed in 1935, provides unsurpassed water, landscape, and wildlife views. Beyond its span, the varied topography of the park also provides viewing opportunities, including seabirds such as cormorants; bald eagles; seals; whales; and porpoises.

Larrabee State Park


245 Chuckanut Drive
Bellingham, WA 98229
(360) 902-8844
www.parks.wa.gov

The only way to reach Larrabee State Park is Chuckanut Drive, which provides a wealth of wildlife and landscape views. Situated on Samish Bay 20 minutes from Bellingham and two hours from Seattle, Larrabee Park has 8,100 feet of saltwater shoreline and more than 2600 acres. The park, which is the state’s oldest, celebrates its 100th anniversary this year. A century later, visitors can still enjoy the unspoiled scenic views of the San Juan Islands and Pacific coast. Visitors will have a chance to see seals and sea lions; birds, including eagles, hawks, herons, and osprey; bobcats; coyotes; foxes; and ungulates, including deer and elk; and marine life, including whales, sea stars, and sea cucumbers.

Paradise, Mount Rainier


39000 State Route 706 E.
Ashford, WA 98304
(360) 569-2211
www.nps.gov

Mount Rainier’s Paradise, which earned its name from an early visitor’s proclamation, “Oh, what a paradise!”, is a haven for plant and animal life alike. Located 19 miles east of Mount Rainier’s Nisqually entrance, Paradise is at an elevation of 5,400 feet. According to the National Park Service, it’s the “snowiest place on Earth where snowfall is measured regularly,” but during temperate weather, it’s also one of the best places to view wildlife. Approximately 126 species of birds, including the Spotted Owl, Little Willow Flycatcher, and Harlequin Duck; 54 species of mammals, including the red fox, black bear, and hoary marmot; and 17 species of amphibians and reptiles live at Mount Rainier National Park.
